Overview of the user-centered design process

Understand & Observe

The Challenge

Many of us have hectic lives! While people may want to learn a new language, it can be hard to find a comprehensive tool they can use on the go. The challenge was to design an accessible mobile vocabulary app that caters to various learning styles. To ensure users can organize new information, study and test their knowledge to minimize studying while maximizing retention on the go.

My process began with thorough competitive analysis. I compared the successes and pain points of three major vocabulary apps in the market. The resulting information was used to survey potential users.

From their responses and nonverbal cues, a user persona was created to embody the wants and needs of my target audience. The information architecture of the app was then created taking into account the user persona's wants and needs. From here, initial wireframes were created which were then iterated upon after a usability test.

To design a vocabulary app that is so fun and useful that people with hectic lives can and want to integrate it into their lives.

competitors

Understanding the Market

I conducted a competitive analysis to understand what is currently on the market and identify opportunities for my own vocabulary app. I choose to analyze three major competitors: Quizlet, Words With Friends, and Magoosh. I chose these apps, because they all had features I proposed.

Through my analysis, I identified an opportunity creating a product that is collaborative, attentive to different learning styles, allows users to organize and study content in a fun and easy way, and can be used on the go.

User Research

I conducted user interviews to gain direct insight into the wants and needs of potential users. I interviewed three individuals with varying experience with vocabulary apps. Interviews were conducted in-person in order to observe nonverbal cues in addition to what was said. Their responses were used to inform future design decisions to ensure a user-centered approach.

POV & Ideate

proto-persona, Sara

Creating my Proto-Personas

Using the data I gathered from my competitor analyses and user interviews, I created a proto-persona I would refer to throughout the design process. Embodying the wants, needs, motivations, and pain points of users was integral as it allowed me to empathize with them more.

Problem

Sara needs a fun and accessible way to effectively organize and manage all the new information and vocabulary she is learning because she must succeed in her classes in order to get her Masters in Business. We will know this to be to be true when Sara succeeds in her classes and gets her MBA.

Hypothesis

We believe that by creating an accessible mobile vocabulary app that caters to different types of learners users, allows users to organize new information, study and test their knowledge, we will minimize the amount of time spent studying while increasing learning effectiveness and retention.

Prototype & Test

Wireframing & Prototyping

Once I had a solid idea of the main content and features of the UWord vocabulary app, I began sketching. I found sketching an effective means of quickly getting my ideas down on paper while leaving room for quick iteration when needed.

To illustrate the app's functionality, I created low-fidelity wireframes for two core features of the app: creating a vocabulary set and mastering a vocabulary set.

I collated the wireframes into a clickable prototype using the online Marvel tool.

Usability Testing

In preparation for the testing of my clickable prototype, I first created a test plan to outline goals and objectives of the test. I wanted to test the app's core features and see where user's encountered pain points as well as success.

I conducted in-person usability sessions with 3 participants.

Final Thoughts

Conclusion

Designing an app from scratch can be very challenging, especially when you believe you do not have any design background. This was my first UX design project, as I am transitioning from the Healthcare industry.

As I moved through the design process, however, I quickly realized my background in Cognitive Science and Neuroscience had equipped me with many skills needed to create a user-centered product. Throughout the design process, I was able to develop the hard skills needed to create useful and intuitive solutions.

I encountered several challenges along the way. From having to learn new design tools like Marvel, finding the balance between fidelity-level and iteration, having to rethink my mental models, and striving for perfection, I was constantly challenged to be adaptable and willing to learn. By keeping the user at the forefront and utilizing outside resources, I was able to overcome these problems and create a product that addresses the wants and needs of my users.

Main Takeaways

01

I would have liked to conduct more interviews/usability tests. This would've given me a better understanding of potential users' needs and provided insight on the product's ability of meeting said need. While there are limits to ROI's with testing, I'd particularly need more usability testing rounds to come to a finalized product

02

It's important to take your time creating your wireframes and prototypes, but it’s just as important to begin testing them. Finding a healthy balance between the two ensures the design process is always being directed by user feedback

03

It's good to guide interviewees and participants, but you want to give them the space to verbalize their thought process. This ensures participants are able to give researchers the comprehensive feedback they need
